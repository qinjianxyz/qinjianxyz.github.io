Exploration and Exploitation

There are many definitions of exploration and exploitation under different contexts. Before we dive into the discussion of exploration and exploitation, I want to clarify the definitions that I will be using in this article. Exploration happens when we try something new without knowing the likely outcome of it. We might have an idea of what could happen, but we do not know for sure. Exploitation happens when we resolve to an old way of doing things because we know for sure how it works. Again, there is no guarantee, and we might be surprised. But generally speaking, exploration offers more uncertainty.

Imagine if you travel to a new city, and you know nothing about the city. In the evening, you want to go out and eat at a restaurant. Which one would you choose? Well, since we have just arrived, assuming that all restaurants have the same ratings, we must take a chance to choose a restaurant that we do not know for sure that we would like. A restaurant can have great ratings and most people absolutely love it, but that restaurant might just not be right for you. This is called exploration. When we explore a city, we want to discover the unknown with the hope that we would find something enjoyable. But what if we are leaving the city tomorrow and this is our last night in town. Would you want to go to a new restaurant or the best restaurant that you have had that you know for sure would give you a good experience. A new restaurant might be even better, but it can also be a lot worse. In this situation, most of us might choose to go the restaurant that we know, and this behavior is called exploitation. We exploit when we do not want to take a chance at finding a better option but instead choose the best option available at the moment.

We can see how time plays an important role in the decision whether we choose to explore or exploit. When we are young, we have nothing to lose, and we are free to explore different industries and different parts of the world. We are more inclined to make new friends because we know we might meet our best friends or someone we really get along in the future. The best choice seems to be in the future. As we get older, we value family time much more. We want to spend time with our family not only because we love our family, but also because we know our family very well. It is difficult to completely surprise someone through people they have known for their entire lives. We are slow to make new friends because we think we have probably made our best friends. It is unlikely that we would meet somebody we like so much more in the future. The best seems to be in the past, not in the future. That is exactly when we start to exploit and stop exploring.

We are not getting more insights on why someone would choose to explore over to exploit. If one believes the best is in the future, one would explore, and vice versa. Let us consider the movie industry. How would the movie industry make decision between exploration and exploitation? If investors invest more in new movies, made of completely anonymous actors, that would be considered as exploration, because the success is purely dependent on the quality of the movie. If the movie itself is not good, and there is no fame, no famous actor, there is no hope to succeed. If investors invest more in sequels, famous actors, in another way, things that are likely to succeed, this would be called exploitation. From the past decade, we can see how sequels have raked heavy profits. What does this tell us? Is the movie industry slowly dying? Does the movie industry believe the best is in the past? If we are conscious about the constant decision between exploration and exploitation, we can make better decisions to maximize our utility.
