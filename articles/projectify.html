Projectify Learning

Why do we learn? Sometimes we learn just for fun, for the sake of it. Many topics are interesting in their own right. I often find myself reading articles on data science and probability even though I have no plan of becoming a data scientist. I sincerely find the content intrinsically interesting and would be potentially helpful for my future work.

With that said, what occurs much more often is that students are trying very hard to learn without understanding why. This is a rampant phenomenon, and I firmly believe it is not given enough serious thoughts that it deserves. The most immediate outcome is that talented and hardworking students find themselves taking class after class and still have no clue on what they could do with the knowledge they obtained through hard work. This phenomenon has bothered me as well, so I have designed a robust system called “projectify learning” to ferociously and relentlessly fight back. The concept of a projectify learning is very simple. “Do something with your knowledge.” In fact, this is not a foreign or even a controversial idea. Most students would agree that they should do more projects involving real world applications to gain more value out of their education. The problem is that there are a lot of misconceptions of projects and many people simply do not know where to start. The goal of this article is to help people in these situations.

“I am just a student and I do not have enough experience to start a project.” Many people have the misconception that one would need 10 years of experience in the industry and at least a master’s degree to start a project. Nothing can be further from the truth. We live in an era of information explosion, and we can learn anything we want basically for free. There are amazing sources of knowledge online, free courses, YouTube, etc. The upside is that anyone with the access to internet can learn. The downside of the is that we will never learn everything. It is actually not a downside but just a reality that we need to live with. The most important thing is just to start doing something. Expecting one would be more prepared to start a project after learning one more thing is like expecting one would become a better driver after biking one more mile.

“Now I understand the benefits of projects and I want to start one, but I do not know where to start.” The best tip I can give is to look for cross-disciplinary opportunities. I have taken a course on material science and for a long time I did not know what I could do it. One day one of my engineer friends complained to me that it takes a long time to find the material she needs to use in her design project. It would be very helpful if a program could take in the parameters she wants and recommend 5 materials that best match her criterion. That clicks with me immediately. “Now, that is an industry problem.” I thought to myself. I quickly identified that the material selector is a multi-disciplinary challenge. It requires knowledge in material science and ultimately it is an optimization problem involving both categorical and numerical parameters, which is a great project idea. There are so more more examples like this where I have connected two seemingly irrelevant fields together to solve an real life problem.

Ultimately, projectify learning is a thinking strategy. It is about taking one step further from the classroom to do something tangible and useful. This thinking strategy definitely needs practice, especially learning without practice is far too common in our educational institutions. With enough practice and understanding of this philosophy, I believe the concept of projectify learning could help individuals become better learners and better doers at the same time.
